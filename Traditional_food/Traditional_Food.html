Kiribath


Kiribath is a popular dish in Sri Lanka made from rice and coconut milk. The name "Kiribath" literally means "milk rice" in the Sinhalese language, which is the predominant language in Sri Lanka. The dish is made by cooking rice in a mixture of coconut milk and water until it becomes thick and creamy. The rice is then spread out on a plate or banana leaf and left to cool and set, before being cut into pieces and served. Kiribath is often eaten for breakfast or during special occasions such as weddings or religious ceremonies. Kiribath can be enjoyed on its own or paired with savory accompaniments such as curries, sambols, or chutneys. It is a versatile dish that can be adapted to suit different tastes and preferences, and is loved by many Sri Lankans for its creamy texture and rich coconut flavor.




Konda Kawum


Konda Kavum is a traditional Sri Lankan sweetmeat made during special occasions such as the Sinhala and Tamil New Year. It is a deep-fried doughnut-shaped snack that is crunchy on the outside and soft on the inside, with a subtle sweetness that comes from the use of jaggery, a type of unrefined cane sugar commonly used in Sri Lankan cuisine. To make konda kavum, rice flour, wheat flour, jaggery, and coconut milk are mixed together to form a batter. The batter is then shaped into small doughnuts and deep-fried in hot oil until golden brown. Konda Kavum is usually served hot, and its crunchy texture and sweet taste make it a favorite among Sri Lankans. Konda Kavum is not only a delicious snack but also holds significant cultural and religious value in Sri Lanka. It is a symbol of prosperity and is often offered to deities during religious ceremonies. The process of making konda kavum is also considered a traditional art form, with many families passing down their unique recipes and techniques from generation to generation.




Aluwa


Aluwa is a traditional Sri Lankan sweet made from rice flour, jaggery, and coconut. It is a popular sweetmeat, especially during festive occasions such as the Sinhala and Tamil New Year. To make aluwa, rice flour is mixed with jaggery syrup and coconut, and the mixture is cooked on low heat until it becomes a thick, sticky dough. The dough is then molded into different shapes, such as diamonds or rectangles, and allowed to cool and set. Aluwa has a chewy, slightly crunchy texture and a sweet taste that comes from the use of jaggery. It is often flavored with cardamom, cinnamon, or other spices and can be decorated with nuts or other toppings. Aluwa is a versatile sweet that can be adapted to suit different occasions and tastes. It can be made in different shapes and sizes, and its flavor can be customized by adding different spices or flavorings. It is also often gifted to friends and family during festive occasions as a symbol of goodwill and prosperity.




Ambul Thiyal


Ambul Thiyal is a popular fish curry dish from the southern coastal regions of Sri Lanka. It is a spicy and tangy dish made with fish, tamarind, and a blend of aromatic spices that give it a unique flavor and aroma. To make ambul thiyal, fish is first marinated in a blend of spices that typically includes turmeric, chili powder, black pepper, and roasted curry powder. The dish is typically served with steamed rice and garnished with fresh herbs and sliced red onions. Ambul Thiyal is a popular dish in Sri Lanka, and it is often prepared during special occasions and festivals.
